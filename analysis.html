Colorado: Haunted By the Ghost of Westward Expansion

Frankie Butler
Dr. Trout
ENL-1895-01
3 May 2026
  *   reading folklore as ideology   *   what the genre confesses   *   what it conceals   *   who gets remembered as a ghost   *   who is allowed to disappear entirely   *  

. * . ☠ . * . ☠ . * . ☠ . * . ☠ . * . ☠ . * . ☠ . * .

For my project, I’ve collected dozens of paranormal stories from across my home state of Colorado. I used three books as my main sources for collecting my stories: Haunted Colorado: Ghosts and Strange Phenomena of the Centennial State by Charles A. Stansfield Jr., Ghost Stories of the Rocky Mountains by Barbara Smith, and Twilight Dwellers: Ghosts, Ghouls, and Goblins of Colorado by MaryJoy Martin. While reading my stories and doing some additional research, I realized quite quickly that many of the stories, in one way or another, are deeply tied to westward expansion. After realizing this, I decided to organize my stories into the five following categories: Hauntings of Conquest; Gold Fever; Outlaws, Vigilantes & the Lawless Frontier; Civilization Imposed; and Boom, Bust & the Personal Cost.

The Hauntings of Conquest category mostly contains stories of the price paid by those who were already in Colorado before Americans started venturing west. Gold Fever speaks for itself, with stories of miners and people who went crazy in the search for gold. Outlaws, Vigilantes & the Lawless Frontier contains some of my favorite stories I’ve come across, including killings, robberies, and rough justice that transpired in the Wild West. The Civilization Imposed category features stories from Hotels, Opera Houses, and other early high-class establishments in Colorado. Finally, Boom, Bust & the Personal Cost is all about what’s left when the frontier moves on, including stories about abandoned towns, family graves, and grief. These categories all work together to paint a much bigger picture.

Many of Colorado’s ghost stories function to support the ideology of Manifest Destiny by acknowledging that violence occurred while avoiding clear responsibility for it. Each of the following stories are from different categories, but they all share a connection to westward expansion. Tipis Out of the Mist aestheticizes a terrible real-life massacre and minimizes the outcomes of westward expansion. One-Armed Ghosts takes dangerous mining conditions and accounts of deaths and turns them into supernatural warnings. Ghosts of Riverdale Road blends different tragedies into one story, ultimately suggesting the inevitability of westward expansion. The Spirit of Buffalo Bill shows how the West was always partly a performance. And Dearfield’s Dear Departed reveals how race affects which stories get remembered and honored.
Robert Lindneaux, "The Sand Creek Massacre" (1936)

Ghost stories often convert documented atrocity into atmospheric folklore. Tipis Out of the Mist is based on the Sand Creek Massacre. In 1864, so-called “Friendly Natives” were told by Colorado’s Governor to go to Fort Lyon where they would receive supplies and protection. The Governor didn’t know it when he sent out his proclamation, but all Military Forts in the territory were instructed to shoot and kill any Native American who approached. Unsure of where to go, Chief Black Kettle decided the 750 Cheyenne and Arapaho people would temporarily settle in a bend of the Big Sandy Creek. The morning of November 29th, Colonel Chivington, commanding members of both the 1st Colorado Infantry Regiment of Volunteers and the 3rd Regiment of Colorado Cavalry Volunteers, opened fire on the indigenous peoples, killing around 230 Cheyenne and Arapaho people (Lindneaux). No one was punished or held accountable for this atrocity. The ghost story Tipis Out of the Mist recognizes this fact, claiming “Possibly because of this lack of justice, the ghosts of the casualties continue to haunt the area” (Smith 126). This story doesn’t intend to justify the mass killing; however, Tipis Out of the Mist effectively depersonalizes the event. Additionally, it’s awfully convenient that the story only discusses the ghosts of the indigenous people who died that day and leaves out the ghosts of Colonel Chivington and the other perpetrators. In her article, “Indigenous spectrality and the politics of postcolonial ghost stories,” Emilie Cameron argues that while ghost stories based on Indigenous atrocities seem like a means to give a voice to the voiceless, “confining the Indigenous to the ghostly also has the potential to re-inscribe the interests of the powerful upon the meanings and memories of place” (Cameron). By turning this tragedy into a ghost story, Tipis Out of the Mist doesn’t quite justify what transpired, but it absolutely minimizes it. This turns a specific act of terrible violence into more of a vague haunting, allowing the story to acknowledge that the tragedy happened while avoiding responsibility and making westward expansion seem like the natural turn of events rather than intentional imperialism.

Cripple Creek mining district, 1898

Similarly, mining stories often minimize mine owners’ negligence by framing workplace accidents as supernatural warnings. In One-Armed Ghosts, the haunting comes from workers who died in mining accidents at mines like the Mamie R in Cripple Creek. The ghosts in this story are often missing arms, legs, or even heads. Rather than focusing on the unsafe working conditions that actually caused these amputations and deaths, the story instead presents these deaths as warnings from beyond the grave. Mining towns, especially in Colorado, have a history of unsafe working conditions and unethical labor practices. In 1894 (around the time the story takes place), members of the Miners Union in Cripple Creek were actively engaged in a battle for fair pay and safer working conditions. It quickly escalated from protest to actual violence, and ultimately, the workers earned only a marginal decrease in their daily hours (Pfeiffer). In “Knockers, Knackers, and Ghosts: Immigrant Folklore in the Western Mines,” the author Ronald James points out that as miners transitioned from being freelance, self-employed individuals to wage-earning employees, mining legends shifted from creatures that help to creatures that warn (James). The One-Armed Ghosts function as a warning, but they also alleviate some of the blame from the people actually responsible for these deaths and amputations. By fostering complacency, this story promotes the capitalist negligence that occurred during westward expansion.

In Ghosts of Riverdale Road, the story combines several deaths from different time periods into a single place. The story starts with Indigenous hunters on foot who are weary and weak from hunger; then jumps forward to a pioneer family digging a shallow grave, only three feet long beside the trail; and ends with a young girl in a pink prom dress killed in a modern car crash. While each of these stories is completely different, because they all took place in the same area, they are presented as part of the same haunting. By itself, this story helps to explain the process of westward expansion. Through its time jumps, Ghosts of Riverdale Road retroactively justifies the ideology of manifest destiny by positioning the road as a unique site where tragedy was destined to occur, rather than a place shaped by specific historical events such as colonization and settlement. In “Indigenous spectrality and the politics of postcolonial ghost stories,” the author points out that settler stories often treat Indigenous people as figures of the past, almost like they were always meant to disappear (Cameron). By placing all these deaths together, the story makes them seem like they are all equally far-fetched, but westward expansion really did happen. By blending different tragedies into a single haunting, the story removes the historical causes behind them and presents violence as something that simply happens near Riverdale Road, reinforcing the idea that westward expansion was unavoidable.

William F. "Buffalo Bill" Cody

Not only was westward expansion not inevitable, but the idea of the “west” was more of a performance than reality, and none of the stories exemplify this better than The Spirit of Buffalo Bill. William F. “Buffalo Bill” Cody is one of the most well-known figures in western folklore. Buffalo Bill’s Wild West show painted an idealized picture of the Wild West. The show featured bronco riding, roping, and shooting, and even helped popularize the word “cowboy” over “cow boy” (A Brief History of William F. "Buffalo Bill" Cody). In the ghost story, Buffalo Bill is still dressed in his show outfit and is said to haunt his grave because he loved attention. Buffalo Bill was instrumental in shaping the mythologized ideology of manifest destiny. Americans already believed in their own superiority and the inferiority of indigenous people, and the mythologization of the West in the form of Buffalo Bill’s Wild West show was all they needed to follow their “destiny.” In the article, “Manifest Mythmaking: The Role of US ‘Manifest Destiny’ in Nineteenth and Twenty-First Century Indigenous Dispossession,” the author says that Manifest Destiny offered “a coordinating vision that linked imperialism and expansionism to the nation’s future in unquestionably positive terms” (Freiin von Bibra). The keyword there is positive. If the national outlook on moving west was negative, westward expansion wouldn’t have occurred. Because people like Buffalo Bill framed manifest destiny as an adventure rather than violent imperialism, many were able to justify their westward travels. The ghost story seems honest about the fact that the “Wild West” was staged. But at the same time, it ignores the real consequences of Buffalo Bill’s mythologization of the West. By focusing on Buffalo Bill as a larger-than-life character rather than his true mortal self, the story continues to celebrate the myth of the Wild West while at the same time downplaying the real violence behind it, helping Manifest Destiny appear exciting and positive rather than destructive.

What remains of Dearfield, Colorado today

The story of Dearfield shows a different side of these ghost stories and reveals how race shapes what gets remembered. The town of Dearfield was established in 1910 by Oliver Toussaint Jackson as an all-Black farming community. From 1917 to 1921, the town had a population of about 300 people and was prosperous by many measures. Sadly, when the Dust Bowl hit Colorado, most of the residents had to move closer to the city of Denver, and eventually Jackson sold the land to the U.S. government (which used the land as a Japanese internment camp). As it sits now, Dearfield is a ghost town (Dearfield Colorado). According to the story, the town’s failure was both “no one’s, and everyone’s” fault (Stansfield Jr.). Much like the previous stories, this one leaves the blame up in the air. What’s unique about this story is what happened to the town after it was vacated. Many of Colorado’s ghost towns have been meticulously historically preserved, but not Dearfield. Despite being the first all-Black town in Colorado, Dearfield has been largely ignored and, in some areas, even redeveloped. In her article, Freiin von Bibra discusses how the ideology of Manifest Destiny functions. She lists three ingredients: divine Providence, the construction of racial superiority and inferiority, and claims of civilizational ‘progress’. Westward expansion is long over, but the ideology of manifest destiny still permeates. The people who built developments where Dearfield once stood might not have felt they were called by god, but they certainly believed in progress, and the decision not to preserve the town seems at least somewhat racially motivated. Dearfield’s story shows that not all histories are valued equally, nor are all losses remembered in the same way. By treating Dearfield’s failure as no one’s fault and failing to preserve the historical area, the story reveals how some histories and spaces are ignored as a result of racism, showing that the legacy of Manifest Destiny still shapes which stories and spaces are remembered and valued.

Based on these stories, it is clear that Colorado is indeed haunted by the ghost of westward expansion. Tipis Out of the Mist depersonalizes a massacre. One-Armed Ghosts supernaturalizes workplace deaths. The Ghosts of Riverdale Road blends tragedies together across time. The Spirit of Buffalo Bill glamorizes a myth-making myth. And Dearfield’s Dear Departed quietly forgets an entire town. All five of these ghost stories perform the same trick: they let the reader feel the weight of violence without ever having to name or even think about who caused it. According to Freiin von Bibra, the ideology behind Manifest Destiny thrives in the absence of accountability. People can look past the horrors of imperialism when blinded by the spirit of adventure. Westward expansion ended over a century ago, but what happened to Dearfield proves that ideology is still alive and well. Unfortunately, wrapping yourself under the covers won’t help you hide from the ghost of westward expansion.
